Colour Schemes

The colour wheel is helpful when trying to decide what colours will be used in a single room or whole home. The closer colours are to each other on the colour wheel the more they blend together and the further apart they are on the colour wheel the more they stand out against one another. There are some common colour scheme names, however not all colours have a scheme name. Without a specific name like red and yellow you can just call it a warm colour scheme or name the colours of the scheme.

Here are some of the more common colour scheme names:

  • Monochromatic
  • Analogous
  • Contrast/Complimentary Scheme
  • Split complimentary
  • Double Complimentary
  • Triad
  • Tetrad
  • Neutral
  • Achromatic

Monochromatic

Mono means one, so a monochromatic scheme is using any one colour family or one long paint strip from the paint store. You can use this one colour from light to dark along with a neutral like white, black, grey, and cream with no undertone.

You can also just use 1 tint, tone or shade in the room if you wish. You can have another colour as a small accent as long as it is less than 5% of the room. For example you could have an accent cushion or flowers and it still be monochromatic.

Analogous

Analogous schemes are 3-4 colours that are side by side on the colour wheel with a common hue; meaning that they are touching one another. Example Red, red-orange and orange (could extend to orange-yellow). The colours can be used up to any 90-degree angle on the colour wheel (4 colours). This scheme allows for the use of multiple colours that blend together. They do not stand out greatly from one another.

This kind of scheme works well for small spaces that work best with blended colours, however can be used in larger rooms as well. You can use from light to dark of each of the 3-4 colours along with neutrals like white, black, grey or a beige with no undertone.

Contrast/Complimentary Scheme

Complimentary also known as contrast schemes are direct opposites on the colour wheel. These colours make each other stand out as they are the highest contrast. You can use from light to dark of each of the 2 colours along with neutrals like white, black, grey or a beige with no undertone.

Using a contrast scheme makes each colour pop. Therefore, an orange looks more orange and a blue bluer when used together.

Example

An apple in a tree looks redder in the tree against the green leaves that once it is picked and in our hand. The green leaf is a high contrast and our hand is peachy or brown colour that is analogous to the red and therefore blends more.

Have you ever noticed at the grocery store that they often have green plastic grass look or bases in their fruit and vegetable area? Why do you think they do this?

They use the green as a marketing tool to make the fruits and vegetables look riper. They do the same at some meat counters that have the dividers made from green plastic grass. The meat looks redder and juicier in the case than in the brown paper wrapping. At some fruit stands you can get peaches in purple paper cups that also make the peaches look riper. This colour scheme is used when you want to accent the colours.

Split complimentary

A split complimentary scheme is similar to the complimentary/contrast scheme, however instead of using the opposite colour you would NOT use that colour and use the colours on each side of the compliment. You can use from light to dark of each of the 3 colours along with neutrals like white, black, grey or a beige with no undertone. This scheme allows for the use of 3 colours at quite high contrast. NOTE: The single colour would be the most prominent colour in the room and the other 2 colours are secondary and accent colours.

Double Complimentary

A double complementary scheme is using 2 complimentary schemes from the colour wheel. This scheme gives you 4 colours to use in a scheme (like a tetrad). This is the maximum number of colours you can use in any one scheme. More colours than this is too confusing in room or home.

You can use from light to dark of each of the 3 colours along with neutrals like white, black, grey or a beige with no undertone.

Triad

A triad colour scheme is any 3 colours on the colour wheel that are equidistant triangles. For example, the primary colours are a triad as is the secondary colours. You can do the same with any 3 colours making the same kind of triangle.

You can use light to dark of each of the 3 colours along with neutrals like white, black, grey or a beige with no undertone.

This scheme gives some contrast but no as much as the contrast, but you get to use 3 colours instead of 2.

Tetrad

A Tetrad scheme is also using 4 colours from the colour wheel in a square or rectangle. See the back of the colour wheel for those shapes that you can match up. This can also be called a double complimentary scheme using 4 colours. 4 is the maximum number of colours you can use in any one scheme. More colours than this is too confusing in room or home. You can use from light to dark of each of the 4 colours along with neutrals like white, black, grey or a beige with no undertone.

Tetrads are a fun way to create colour and enjoyment, especially in outdoor furniture these days.

Neutral

A neutral scheme is using cream, and brown tones like tan, chocolate and the like. You can use no more than 5% of a punch colour in the room (not on drapery or furniture) as an accent.

You can use from light to dark of each of the 3 colours along with neutrals like white, black, grey or a beige with no undertone.

Achromatic

Achromatic schemes are non-colour schemes like white, black and grey that have no identifiable hue. You can use no more than 5% of a punch colour in the room (not on drapery or furniture) as an accent. You can use from light to dark of each of the tint, tone and shade along with a beige with no undertone.
