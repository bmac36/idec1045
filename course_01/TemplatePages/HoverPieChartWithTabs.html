Psychology of colour

To understand how we interpret certain colours we will discuss the 6 main colour families on the colour wheel…primary and secondary colours.

Red: Primary colour

  • Associated with danger, action, anger, passion and romance
  • Terminology like: “I am so angry I am seeing red”, red alert
  • Heart rate increases in this colour room and therefore blood pressure increases resulting in an adrenalin rush that makes people feel warmer
  • Associate lots of foods like apples, cherries, tomatoes, red peppers, red meats
  • Hottest colour on the colour wheel
  • Bright pure red is the most demanding colour on the colour wheel
  • Looks great with neutrals like white, black, grey and taupe

Pink: Tint of primary red (red and white)

  • Including as is relatively new colour in decorating and part of the red colour family
  • Became mainstream in 1950’s
  • Associated with feminine, little girls, Barbie, lipstick, innocence, romance
  • In pastel shades can be good bedroom colour
  • Baker Miller Pink = specific colour used in prisons and mental institutions to calm violent inmates. Learn more about Baker Miller Pink here: Baker-Miller Pink opens in new window

Practice advice: Pink, like red is a strong demanding colour. Be careful not to select a colour that is too intense as it can end up looking like Pepto Bismol on your walls. I suggest that once you find the pink you like, select a shade a couple of levels lighter. It will end up looking like the colour you want and not too intense once painted on the wall.

Orange: Secondary colour, mix of red and yellow

  • Still a hot colour but not quite as hot as red
  • Full strength is brash and bold
  • Adult colour. Liked by those under 30 as they have not seen this colour much in decorating
  • Children don’t tend to like this colour as much
  • Associated with speed and action, sales and advertising, construction, fall leaves, pumpkins, peppers, oranges, melons
  • Great modern colour when mixed with brown or other neutrals

Yellow: Primary colour

  • Golden glow of sun
  • Most visible colour on colour wheel
  • Associated with caution fear (yellow belly), sunlight, daffodils, butter, lemons
  • Intensity can cause fatigue on eyes like a flash bulb so use less intensity when decorating a room
  • Great transition colour in decorating.
  • Good colour for rooms with little or no windows or natural light

Green: Secondary colour, mix of yellow and blue

  • Most peaceful of all colours on the colour wheel that is easy on the eyes
  • Green rooms used in theatre and TV to rest eyes of those after they are on stage under bright yellow lights.
  • Colour of balance and wellbeing and healing
  • Symbolizes hope and joy of spring, fertility and sexuality
  • Associated with green leafy vegetables, grass, leaves and shrubs
  • Good performing colour in offices, school rooms
  • Some kind of green always in decorating with either yellow-greens or blue greens

Blue: Primary colour

  • Coolest colour on the colour wheel
  • Calming colour that lowers the blood pressure
  • Associated with cleanliness, trust (true blue), authority, royalty (blue blood), summer sky, lakes and oceans, denim
  • Was 1st colour manufactured synthetically from semi-precious stone Lapis Lazuli and cost as much as gold
  • Check this website opens in new window for more information on this stone
  • By 15th Century it was used in Chinese porcelain and through trade brought to England to Flow blue china and Holland with Delft Blue wares.
  • Blue colour decreases appetite. Blue foods not appealing other than blueberries. We don’t like blue spaghetti for example!

Violet: Secondary colour, mix of blue and red

  • Not as common colour in decorating, however popular in middle of 2010 decade
  • Not seen as often as other colours on colour wheel in decorating
  • Symbol of religious vestments, mourning, wealth and sensuality
  • Associated with plums, eggplant, makeup colours
  • Looks great with gold, taupe and tan colours and warm grays
  • Soft Lavender is good bedroom colour

Overall all colours go together and can be used in almost any combination. Knowing what colours you like to live in should be surrounding you in your home. When we are around the colours we enjoy, we are more comfortable. The colours we select for our home may not be the same as the ones we wear. We will often wear more colour than we will put in our home.
